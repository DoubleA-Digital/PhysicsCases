📖 Story

During a controlled test launch, a prototype rocket with a mass of 200 kg was fired straight upward with an initial velocity of 80 m/s. According to the pre-launch simulation, the rocket should have reached a predictable maximum height. But mid-flight telemetry showed the rocket only reached 280 meters before descending — well below the expected altitude. Engineers suspect energy was lost somewhere in the system. Your job is to figure out exactly what happened and how much was lost.

📚 Full Solution

Step 1 — Predicted Height
Using energy conservation (KE = PE): ½mv² = mgh
→ h = v²/(2g) = 80²/(2 × 9.8) = 6400/19.6 ≈ 326.5 m
This is the height the rocket should have reached with no energy loss.
Step 2 — Energy Lost
Initial KE = ½mv² = ½ × 200 × 80² = 640,000 J
PE at actual height = mgh = 200 × 9.8 × 280 = 548,800 J
Energy lost = 640,000 − 548,800 = 91,200 J
Step 3 — Percentage Lost
% lost = (91,200 / 640,000) × 100 ≈ 14.25%
About 14% of the rocket's kinetic energy was lost to drag and heat.

Step 1: Predicted Height

Hint 1
Use conservation of energy: all kinetic energy converts to potential energy at max height.
Hint 2
KE = PE → ½mv² = mgh → h = v²/(2g). Plug in v = 80, g = 9.8.
Step 2: Energy Lost
The rocket actually reached 280 m. How many joules of energy were lost compared to the ideal case?
J
Hint 1
Find the initial kinetic energy: KE = ½mv². Then find the PE gained at 280 m: PE = mgh.
Hint 2
Energy lost = Initial KE − PE at actual height. KE = ½(200)(80²) = 640,000 J. PE = 200 × 9.8 × 280.
Step 3: Percentage Lost
What percentage of the rocket's initial kinetic energy was lost? Round to 2 decimal places.
%
Hint 1
Percentage = (energy lost / initial energy) × 100
Hint 2
Use your Step 2 answer. Divide it by 640,000 J (initial KE) and multiply by 100.
